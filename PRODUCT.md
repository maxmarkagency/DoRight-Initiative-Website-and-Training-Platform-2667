# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

Two audiences matter equally, and neither should feel like an afterthought. **Prospective participants and beneficiaries** arrive looking for civic-integrity programs, campaigns, or training, trying to understand what's available and how to get involved. **Donors, partners, and funders** arrive evaluating DoRight's credibility and impact to decide whether to support or partner with the organization. The public site has to work for both without picking a side — a participant shouldn't feel like they've wandered into a funder pitch deck, and a funder shouldn't feel like they've landed on a program brochure with no evidence of rigor.

## Product Purpose

DoRight Foundation's public site exists to build trust before it asks for anything. Rather than pushing for immediate conversion, the primary job of a first visit is for the visitor to come away believing the organization is real, legitimate, and doing substantive work — trust as the foundation success metric, with exploration and enrollment as what follows once that trust is established.

## Positioning

DoRight (Doing Right Awareness Initiative) is a civic-integrity and accountability foundation, not a training catalog with a foundation attached. Its core mission is promoting integrity, accountability, and civic responsibility across Nigeria through community campaigns, public education, and policy advocacy; skills training and certification are one program among several, not the headline identity. The differentiator is depth of commitment to the community it serves, not breadth of program offerings. Every page should reinforce that this is an organization embedded in the community, not delivering services to it from a distance.

## Conversion & proof

- Primary and secondary CTA: Explore programs (primary) → Contact / partner inquiry (fallback for visitors not ready to commit to a program).
- The line a visitor remembers after 10 seconds: "DoRight is a foundation genuinely rooted in the community it serves — building a culture of integrity, accountability, and civic responsibility, not just running campaigns."
- Belief ladder: (1) this organization is real and legitimate, (2) they understand the community's actual needs — not generic charity theater, (3) their programs lead to tangible outcomes in the community, (4) exploring costs nothing and could open a real way to get involved.
- Proof on hand: none yet. The site should not lean on placeholder testimonials, fabricated logos, or stock "success story" imagery to manufacture credibility it doesn't yet have on hand — let program specifics and institutional clarity carry credibility until real proof exists to add.

## Brand Personality

Bold, dignified, ambitious. This is a foundation with real momentum and high standards, not a scrappy small charity leaning on sympathy. Confident and forward-looking, without tipping into corporate or detached.

## Anti-references

Should not read as a generic startup/SaaS site — no gradient hero, no rounded-card grid, no "trusted by" logo strip performing legitimacy it hasn't earned. Should also not read as a government or bureaucratic site — no dense, dry, form-heavy pages with no visual identity, nothing that feels like paperwork rather than a mission.

## Design Principles

Community-rootedness over institutional distance: every page should read as embedded in the community it serves, never as an organization observing or serving from above. Dignity and ambition over charity-cliché: present the mission and the people it serves with the same confidence and polish as any ambitious, credible institution — no poverty framing, no pity appeals. Earn credibility with substance, not simulated proof: since testimonials and press don't exist yet, don't fabricate the appearance of social proof — let specificity about programs and impact carry the weight instead. Serve both audiences without diluting either: a trainee exploring programs and a funder evaluating credibility should each find what they came for on the same pages. One institution, one visual language: the public brand and the LMS dashboard are the same organization; the product-register dashboard should still feel like it belongs to this brand, even though its own design decisions are governed separately.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.2 AA as the baseline: color contrast, full keyboard navigation, screen reader support, and respect for reduced-motion preference.
